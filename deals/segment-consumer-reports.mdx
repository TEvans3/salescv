## How It Started

Inbound signal via Segment's self-serve motion — Cody Juric, Sr. Growth Product Manager, had used Segment at three prior companies (Cover Hound, Propeller Health, Section 4) and had been brought into CR's core product team after their Upkept app was sunset. He was now Product Lead for Data & Personalization and had identified a clear gap in CR's stack: no real-time data router, no unified customer profile, and a 24–72 hour delay getting data into downstream tools. He reached out to Segment because he believed it was the solution — I had to work with him to build the internal business case and navigate the org to get it bought.

## Technical Validation

The POC ran approximately June–October 2025, executed jointly by Marcin Czauz (Web Engineering) and Marcelo Arazi (Data Engineering) on CR's side, with Nishant Sheth (Principal SE) and Andy Cogbill (Sr. Principal Staff Solutions Advisor) as the Segment SE team. I personally ran the 5/22/25 full-day onsite demo workshop in Yonkers — a structured session covering current state recap, personalization use-case demo, POC requirements demo, and architecture design discussion.

**Six validated use cases:**
1. **Event Routing & Schema Enforcement** — Protocols tracking plans for web, iOS, Android, server-side. Violations blocked.
2. **Deterministic ID Resolution** — Stitched anonymous IDs, member IDs, newsletter signup IDs, ECQ IDs, print subscriber IDs into unified profiles via Unify ID Graph.
3. **Reverse ETL from Databricks** — Pulled `donor_tier` and `member_since` from Databricks into Segment Profiles as computed traits. `member_since` reconciled across CDH (Acxiom) and digital sources.
4. **Cross-Device Homepage Personalization** — Profile API queried for `most_recent_model_theme` computed trait; Marcin wrote personalization code to serve dynamic homepage experience based on this trait in near real-time.
5. **Mobile Push via Braze** — `Interest Selected` event from mobile source delivered to Braze; triggered personalized push notification. Confirmed delivery.
6. **DWH Profile Sync** — Unified profiles written back to Databricks. Confirmed `Signed Up` events and profile data in Databricks.

Architecture design session covered three CDP patterns: Reverse ETL, Linked Events, and Linked Audiences — all three ran compute on Databricks, which was the decisive alignment point with Suman's data team.

## Negotiation Tactics

**Starting position**: $250K ARR, 2-year term, Connections + Protocols + Unify + Signature Services.

**First concession**: Reduced to $400K (20% savings / $50K off) in exchange for 2-year commitment and 9/30 execution deadline — Signature Services effectively "free" vs. list price. This was the proposal sent to Cody in writing.

**Key objection**: Suman wanted a 1-year term. New CTO was unknown. Deloitte assessment was in-flight. Couldn't commit to 2 years without both resolved.

**Counter**: Offered 1-year term at $400K — same price, shorter commitment. This removed the primary hesitancy without sacrificing ARR rate. The trade was term length (from Twilio's perspective, losing 1 year of locked ARR) in exchange for getting the deal signed in the current quarter before Cody went on paternity leave and before a new CTO could restart evaluation.

**Legal negotiation**: Two-week fight over liability cap. I structured the resolution: cap set at *the greater of $2M or 5x annual contract value*, with a carve-out making the cap inapplicable in cases where Twilio's breach of its information security obligations caused the claim. This was a principled concession — CR's concern was legitimate (they're a nonprofit with member data, not a for-profit enterprise), and meeting it closed the deal.

**API calls vs. MTU**: CR was initially being quoted on an MTU basis. I modeled the comparison, confirmed API Call pricing was $0.000167/unit vs. $0.0089/unit MTU equivalent — 53x cheaper per unit — and delivered this in writing to Cody and Suman to close the pricing objection.

## Competitive Differentiation

The decisive strategic move in this deal was the **Databricks partnership play**. When I learned that Suman's Data Office was evaluating Segment vs. build-in-house, and that their entire data strategy was centered on Databricks (migrating off Snowflake, building toward Unity Catalog), I brought in Segment's Databricks partner team. The message: every Linked Audience computation, every Profile Sync write, every rETL job would run as compute on Databricks — meaning Segment was *driving Databricks workloads*, not competing with them. This reframed Segment from "another tool to integrate" to "the activation layer for your warehouse strategy."

The second decisive move was the **best-of-breed vs. suite argument**. When Adobe CDP was raised as an alternative, I didn't compete on features — I used their own stakeholders as the argument. Nahrae in Marketing had said it clearly: *"85 years of scientific research data, AskCR, mobile app differentiation, Braze for push — no Suite is going to do any of that."* I brought that framing back into every executive conversation.
